You've hit on one of the most crucial points in learning to code: **active practice vs. passive consumption.**

Here's the breakdown of what you should do:

### The Short Answer:
**You should absolutely do BOTH, but practice by yourself (actively coding) is far more important and where real learning happens.** Watching YouTube videos alone is like watching someone play a sport – you understand the rules, but you won't become good until you get on the field yourself.

### Why Watching YouTube Videos is Good (and How to Use Them Effectively):

**Pros:**
* **Initial Understanding:** Videos are excellent for getting a quick overview of a concept, seeing how things are done, and understanding the "why" behind certain techniques.
* **Demonstrations:** You can see code being written in real-time, how browsers render it, and common mistakes to avoid.
* **Inspiration:** Videos can give you ideas for projects or design patterns.
* **Guidance for Guided Projects:** Some channels offer "build this project with me" videos, which can be a good starting point if you're feeling stuck.

**Cons (and how to avoid them):**
* **"Tutorial Hell":** The biggest trap! You watch video after video, feel like you're learning, but when you sit down to code on your own, you can't remember anything. This is because you're not engaging your brain in problem-solving.
* **Passive Learning:** Your brain isn't forming the necessary connections or muscle memory that comes from active typing and debugging.

**How to Use YouTube Videos Effectively for HTML/CSS Practice:**
1.  **Don't Just Watch, DO:**
    * **Code Along:** Pause the video frequently. Type out every single line of code the instructor types. Don't copy-paste. This builds muscle memory.
    * **Experiment:** Before the instructor explains the next step, try to predict what they might do, or try a different approach yourself to see if it works.
    * **Break It:** Deliberately change values or syntax to see what errors occur. This helps you understand why the correct way works.
2.  **Treat Them as Starting Points:** Use a video to learn a new concept (e.g., "how to use CSS Grid"). Once the video is over, immediately try to build something *different* using that concept, without looking back at the video unless absolutely necessary.
3.  **Focus on Concepts, Not Just Copying:** Understand *why* certain HTML tags are used or *why* a particular CSS property is applied.

### Why Practicing By Yourself is Essential:

This is where the real learning, problem-solving, and skill development happens.

* **Problem-Solving:** When you code by yourself, you will inevitably encounter errors. Learning to debug these errors is a critical skill that you won't develop just by watching.
* **Retention:** Actively typing, making mistakes, and fixing them embeds the knowledge in your brain far better than just observing.
* **Deep Understanding:** You'll gain a much deeper understanding of how HTML elements interact and how CSS properties cascade and override each other.
* **Confidence Building:** Each time you successfully build something from scratch, your confidence grows.
* **Portfolio Building:** The projects you build yourself are what you'll show potential employers.

### What You Need to Do for Effective Self-Practice:

1.  **Set Up Your Environment:**
    * **Code Editor:** Get a good code editor like VS Code (Visual Studio Code). It's free, powerful, and has great extensions for web development.
    * **Browser:** You already have one (Chrome, Firefox, Edge, etc.).
    * **Folder Structure:** Create a dedicated folder on your computer for your projects. Inside it, for each project, create a folder containing your `index.html`, `style.css`, and any image folders.

2.  **Start Small and Build Consistently:**
    * **Replicate:** After watching a video or tutorial, try to recreate the exact same project from memory.
    * **Small Challenges:** Give yourself tiny, specific tasks:
        * "Build a header with a logo and navigation links."
        * "Create three product cards in a row using Flexbox."
        * "Design a simple contact form."
        * "Make a button change color when hovered."
    * **Break Down Designs:** Look at websites you admire. Pick a small section (e.g., just the footer, a specific card, or a navigation bar) and try to replicate *only that section* using HTML and CSS.
    * **Build Mini-Projects:**
        * A personal portfolio page
        * A simple blog layout
        * A landing page for a fictional product
        * A basic photo gallery
        * A simple pricing table

3.  **Embrace the Developer Tools in Your Browser:**
    * Right-click on any webpage and select "Inspect" (or "Inspect Element").
    * Spend time in the "Elements" tab to see the HTML structure and change CSS styles live.
    * Use the "Console" for JavaScript errors (you'll get there!).
    * This is your best friend for debugging and understanding how professional sites are built.

4.  **Google is Your Best Friend:**
    * When you get stuck, google your error messages exactly.
    * Google "how to center a div in CSS" or "HTML table best practices." Learning how to find answers is a crucial developer skill.

**In summary:** Use YouTube videos to *learn* concepts and get inspiration. Then, immediately switch to active, hands-on coding to *apply* and *solidify* that knowledge. Build small, build often, and don't be afraid to break things!
